In the database lab, our teacher asked us to choose a dataset from Kaggle and design our own dataset work around it. I took that seriously because I wanted to do something that felt useful instead of only ordinary. Within one week I researched a Chronic Kidney Disease predictor dataset and settled on it because the problem felt real and important. The dataset had 24 records with useful features, and that was enough for me to start shaping the project in a practical way.

The goal was to check whether a patient had CKD or not. I used a logistic regression classifier for the prediction part, and then I built a FastAPI backend around it so the system could respond properly. When I finally saw the model working, it felt satisfying in a way that simple lab submission never could. The result was close to 90 percent accuracy, which gave me confidence that the work had moved beyond a classroom exercise and into something that looked and behaved like a real solution.

What made this even more meaningful was the way Dr. Bilal Ahmad had been training us to think. In his Programming Fundamentals and Database Systems classes, he kept pushing us toward real-world problems, and he often encouraged the use of medical datasets because they are usually collected with a high level of precision and accuracy. That habit changed my own thinking, because I stopped looking for easy sample data and started looking for problems that actually matter. His teaching style was a big reason why I became more serious about machine learning work.

At the same time, we were also dealing with DLD and the rest of the semester pressure. In the DLD lab, we built a fire and smoke alarm detection system, and that project worked properly too. I also had a GUI game project that turned out well, so the semester kept mixing practical coding with deadlines from different directions. Everything was moving at once, and I had to keep adapting.

The final week before exams was probably the hardest week of my life. I had at least four quizzes and a huge number of labs to finish, including 14 for Database, 14 for OOP, and 14 for DLD. I did not get enough sleep, my health was poor, and I could feel the pressure physically. Because the semester had been delayed, we also did not get the kind of gap or holidays that students usually hope for before finals. We went into the exams almost continuously, without much breathing space.

Even with that pressure, I still look back at the semester with pride. I learned a lot in a very short time, and I learned it in a way that felt earned. The grade matters, the marks matter, but the bigger lesson was that I could work under stress and still produce something useful. That is the kind of progress I want to keep building. #MLwithDrBilalAhmad #DrBilalAhmad #MLProject
