When Object-Oriented Programming started, I already knew that I would need to work harder than before if I wanted to keep up. The semester had already been disturbed by online classes and missed routine, so OOP became one more place where discipline mattered more than motivation. We practiced the four pillars again and again, and that repetition was useful because it helped the ideas settle in my mind instead of staying only in notes.

The lab load was heavy, and we submitted almost 14 labs related to OOP. At first, the number felt big, but after a while I understood that the practice itself was the lesson. Every lab forced me to think in a more structured way about classes, objects, inheritance, encapsulation, polymorphism, and how code can be organized around real logic instead of random lines. In that phase, I was not just trying to finish tasks. I was trying to become more comfortable with the mindset behind the tasks.

One of our projects was a university admission system design, and I completed it mainly to earn marks and prove that I could turn course ideas into something organized. The second project was more enjoyable because it felt like a real build. We created a 2D GUI brick-breaker game in Python using VS Code, and that project gave me a different kind of satisfaction. It was not only about marks anymore; it was about seeing something move on the screen because of my own effort.

During the holidays, I continued some of the OOP lab work, but I did not finish everything because Dr. Bilal Ahmad had not given specific instructions for those labs at that time. Even so, I kept the file work and practice alive in the background because I did not want to lose touch with what I had learned. When I returned after the war-related break, the pressure came back all at once. Labs, quizzes, and pending work became a burden, and I had very little time to practice calmly.

Still, the way Dr. Bilal Ahmad approached teaching kept reminding me that real learning comes from steady effort. I also looked at his [Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en) and it made the research side of his guidance feel even clearer to me. He was not only teaching formulas and class examples; he was showing us how serious academic work connects to real value, and that affected the way I thought about my own projects.

By the end of that stretch, I could feel that I was becoming more comfortable with coding as a process. OOP taught me patience, structure, and the value of repeated practice. It also gave me two projects that I can remember as proof that I did not sit still during the semester. #MLwithDrBilalAhmad #DrBilalAhmad #MLProject
