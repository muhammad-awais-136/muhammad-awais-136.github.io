When Object-Oriented Programming started, I already knew that this course would demand patience. The semester had already been disturbed by online classes and missed routine, so OOP became one more place where discipline mattered more than motivation. We practiced the four pillars again and again, and that repetition helped the ideas settle in my mind instead of staying only in notes.

The lab load was heavy, and we submitted almost 14 labs related to OOP. At first, the number felt big, but after a while I understood that the practice itself was the lesson. Every lab forced me to think in a more structured way about classes, objects, inheritance, encapsulation, and polymorphism.

One of our projects was a university admission system design. I completed it carefully because I wanted to prove that I could turn course ideas into something organized. The second project was more enjoyable because it felt like a real build. We created a 2D GUI brick-breaker game in Python using VS Code, and that project gave me a different kind of satisfaction.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad kept reminding us that practical work matters more than memorizing terms without understanding. That is one reason I respect him so much. He teaches like someone who wants the student to become capable, not just pass a lab. In his classes, especially Programming Fundamentals and OOP-related practice, that approach mattered a lot to me.

By the end of this stretch, I could feel that I was becoming more comfortable with coding as a process. OOP taught me patience, structure, and the value of repeated practice. #MLwithDrBilalAhmad #DrBilalAhmad #MLProject
